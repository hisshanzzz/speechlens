Distinguished guests, dear friends.

Imagine a room where memories fade like old photographs. I spent more than fifty hours in such rooms, volunteering with the Lanka Alzheimer's Foundation. I sat with people who could not remember my name from one visit to the next. Yet every week, they remembered something far more important. They remembered how a kind voice made them feel.

Volunteering taught me a lesson no classroom ever did. Service is not about grand gestures. It is about showing up.

I have shown up at literary festivals before sunrise to arrange chairs. I have shown up at cricket stadiums to guide thousands of fans to their seats. I have shown up at night runs, handing water to strangers who finished their race long after the cameras left. None of these moments will ever appear on the news. All of them changed me.

There is a question young people ask me often. Why should I volunteer when I am not paid? Here is my answer. Volunteering pays you in a currency that no employer can print. It pays you in perspective.

When you serve someone living with dementia, you learn patience. When you manage a crowd of impatient fans, you learn calm. When you edit an article for a peace organization at midnight, you learn that your skills mean nothing until they are useful to someone else.

Sri Lanka is a small island with an enormous heart. Every community here carries stories of neighbors feeding neighbors during the hardest years. We inherited that spirit. The question is whether we will pass it on.

So tonight, I will not ask you to change the world. I will ask you for one hour. Find one cause within walking distance of your home and give it one honest hour this month. The world changes one hour at a time.

The people in those fading rooms taught me that we are not remembered for what we kept. We are remembered for what we gave. Thank you.
